Tomography: the principle

Interactions of X-ray with matter

X-ray beam is attenuated when X-ray photons are removed from the beam by interaction with matter. This can happen in 4 different ways:

  • Photo-electric absorption
  • Compton scattering
  • Thomson (Rayleigh) scattering
  • Pair production

In practice, only the first three interactions may be of importance in X-ray CT

It is not possible to differentiate between these interaction processes directly. Only the intensity (I) of the X-ray beam passing through the object is detected.

Tomography: the principle

Lambert-Beer law

Measured intensity at detector plane is given by the Lambert-Beer law

μ= linear attenuation coefficient of the object

s = thickness of the object

Lambert-Beer law

Materials with a high linear attenuation coefficient allow X-rays to penetrate only a relatively short distance

Materials with a low attenuation coefficient are more easily penetrated

What do we measure?

Partial volume effect

  • CT slices represent certain thickness: « voxels » or volume elements (cfr. pixels)
  • Voxel gray value represents « average attenuation coefficient » in that volume
  • Voxels at boundaries will have a (weighted) average gray value
  • Pores smaller than voxel size will not be observed as pores, but as local decrease in grayvalue
  • Measured attenuation coefficient depends on macroscopic density

Partial volume effect

What do we measure?

Grey value µ(x,y,z): local attenuation coefficient

  • Strongly depending on atomic number
  • Proportional to mass density
  • Energy dependent (µ lower if E higher)
